Statement Of Intent

This project seeks to delve into how everyday online interactions are being used to generate real economic value, without compensating the user who generates this income. It investigates how cookie consent systems are manipulatively designed to frame unpaid digital labour as convenience and a choice, raising ethical questions about responsibility in digital design.

Developing the Research Question

Settling on a research question proved to be an iterative process. My focus was clear from early on — the relationship between cookie consent, user tracking, and the idea that everyday online behaviour constitutes a form of unpaid labour — but pinning down the exact framing took some deliberation.

My first draft took a more measured, academic angle:

"To what extent do consent-based tracking systems take advantage of online behaviour as unpaid labour, in the digital world?"

This version works well analytically — the phrase "to what extent" invites a nuanced, graduated response rather than a binary one. However, I felt it leaned slightly too passive, positioning the user as someone things happen to, rather than someone actively (if unknowingly) participating in a system.

My second iteration shifted the focus toward transformation and agency:

"How does consent-based tracking transform online behaviour into unpaid labour in the digital world?"

The word "transform" felt more dynamic and better captured the mechanism I was interested in — the process by which something as mundane as clicking "Accept All" becomes economically productive activity for a third party. This version also puts the tracking system itself under scrutiny, rather than simply measuring the scale of its impact.

A third option I explored was broader and more accessible in tone:

"Is there a level of unpaid labour that we unknowingly accept, in the digital world?"

While this framing is more conversational and relatable to a general audience, it felt too open-ended for the specific focus of this project. The word "unknowingly" also sits in slight tension with consent-based systems, where users are — technically, if not meaningfully — presented with a choice.

Ultimately, the second question felt like the strongest fit: specific enough to guide focused research, but open enough to explore the ethical, design, and economic dimensions of the topic. It became the foundation for the rest of the project.

Final research question: "How does consent-based tracking transform online behaviour into unpaid labour in the digital world?"
